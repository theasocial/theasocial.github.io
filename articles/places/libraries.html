Introduction

Never before it was so easy to share knowledge: currently it takes moments and costs approximately nothing to send a book copy to the other side of the planet. You can just get a lot of books on any topic, skim those and look for suitable ones, or pick random ones to read, or find the ones you need – better than any offline library, not only in that you can do it anytime and from any computer connected to the internet.

Unfortunately, computers are not always connected to the internet, search engines tend to throw fraud and commercial sites instead of books, some governments block libraries, and others do threat to put you into a jail with rape for “piracy”. Not a big deal, but not particularly convenient; better to know where the books are.

Project Gutenberg

Project Gutenberg is a nice library, especially for recreational reading: it is all legal, hence books get there when their copyright expires; a lot of useful information gets outdated by that time. For offline reading (as well as for backup purposes), it provides CD images, though even less books are there.

There are somewhat similar libraries, e.g. The Online Books Page, but usually it’s even harder to find anything useful there.

Wikibooks

Wikibooks is a very promising project, yet there is very few finished books by now – when it comes to maths, at least. Since even those are not guaranteed to be the best for you, it is not the most useful library for now; hopefully, it will be in the future.

Wikipedia

Though Wikipedia is not a library, its printed version would consist of a couple of thousands of volumes, which would make a small library. The best part here is that it’s a great encyclopedia, which you can download – to be prepared for a zombie apocalypse, censorship of Wikipedia, internet connection issues; or simply to have lower latency.

BitTorrent trackers

The Pirate Bay (thepiratebay.se, thepiratebay.gd), Kickass Torrents, as well as specialized search engines, such as torrentz.eu, may help you to find many books; often it is easier to find a specific book there, than by using a general-purpose web search engine.

Offline libraries

The thing about offline libraries is that you can pick, skim, and read anything, anytime, and for free; it can also be written onto a device that fits into your pocket. Unfortunately, it is too good to be legal, so we won’t risk to provide the links. But here is a few names of mathematical libraries:

  • The MYRIAD Mathematical Library: around 11 GB of pure (and applied) awesomeness.
  • -- mit math: 2 GB of that.
  • MATH MATHEMATICS BOOK COLLECTION: almost 10 GB.

Tor network libraries

The Hidden Wiki has a page for libraries; though one may think that anonymity leads to great libraries, in fact it is not necessarily the case. Yet, there are nice ones, Clockwise Libraries in particular.
